5 things I learned in my Android internship at TeleSoftas

Hey, I’m Justas Gasparaitis, in this story, I will reveal 5 things I learned during the time I spent at my Android internship at TeleSoftas.

I had been learning Android development all by myself for two years when I finally landed the internship at TeleSoftas.

I am a highly passionate person and all I needed was a professional push-forward, which is exactly what I got at TeleSoftas.

The 5 Things I Learned

1. Completing the onboarding task prepares you for the real world ahead, equipped with the best practices needed.

All the tools required to learn to complete building the onboarding app are mostly everything that’s needed for a developer to be prepared to contribute to a large project.

The tools were industry-standard and I was required to implement them using industry best practices, which is one thing that is so, so hard to learn by yourself on the internet.

Sometimes, even the official documentation may not provide all the things that you need, and that is the greatest thing I learned — best practices.

All of the tools required for use are up-to-date and extremely commonly used in the Android ecosystem, and I applied every single one of them successfully!

2. Code reviews are awesome. (if you can take it!)

Once upon a time, back in the wild days of learning to code, that’s how I named my commits.

Before you choke on your laughter, let me explain.

I can recall being stuck on a problem for days on end when I finally fixed it.

I must’ve rolled my head on the keyboard out of excitement right after.

Come on, we’ve all been there, I have learned from my mistakes.

Anyway — code reviews!

It’s a real shame, but commits named ‘adsjlfnadsjkvzxcnlvjkarf’ won’t work.

It’s not your own project, where anything goes, so you must abide by the rules.

And that’s a great thing because every little thing you write will be held up against a standard, which is exactly what you want when learning to code in a clean way.

Sharpen your blade, it’s time to start fighting for your pull requests.

3. The experienced are excited to share knowledge and lessons with the less experienced.

Complicated topics were explained by the team with the help of meetings.

Let’s run through some meetings that I had:

  • Daily standup. I had a daily standup with my mentor Mon-Fri, 9.00 am to 9.30 am. During the standup, I could ask for help on tasks I was struggling with and ask any questions that were bothering me.
  • 1–2–1. It’s a type of regular meeting held between a manager and their employee that involves discussing growth, performance, development, salary, motivation, etc.

We also had some meetings about complicated or fresh topics, such as:

  • Git, where we were taught about commits, branching, merging, etc.
  • Jetpack Compose, a brief introduction to Compose and what it is (I had only known XML before the internship),
  • Agile and Scrum, what they mean and how they are implemented,
  • GraphQL, how it is used in real Android projects with Apollo,
  • Flutter, an introduction to the ever-growing cross-platform framework, powered by Google.

The meetings were held by a lot of different members of the team, so everybody from the Android team kindly participated in sharing some of their knowledge.

As I was a part of the Android team, there were:

  • Android Dojo, where we played coding games, completed challenges, etc.
  • Android Standup, a monthly update on how every platform member is doing.

Let’s not forget that a developer is not someone who only codes all day!

Communication is key and we also had a meeting with one of our mentors on how to talk to & deal with people.

4. Large codebases are not as intimidating as they seem.

Yeah, I had the chance to participate in a large project!

So, it was the first time I laid my hands upon a sizable codebase.

That was an especially important moment for me, as it was the numero uno thing I was seeking to gain from the internship.

I was also introduced to the magnificent world of Gradle builds.

All jokes aside, when I was accepted into the project, I was mostly treated as a full team member, even though I was an intern, which was heart-warming.

Coding a small onboarding app and implementing a feature in a giant codebase are things that need entirely different skill sets.

Some people say, there aren’t many things that can beat the feeling of having your first piece of code published to production and used successfully by users.

I remember having a wide smile on my face after having my moment.

5. You are a part of TeleSoftas during your internship, just like everyone is.

I won’t lie, I never thought programmers knew how to have fun.

Trust me, I couldn’t be more wrong.

There were events for interns, such as Fun Night, where all the interns hung out & played games.

There were huge parties like Blaster Fest, Matrix Party… the list goes on. Those were the most fun ones, I danced my heart out.

Here we had a Mobile Auction, where we bid imaginary points and competed to win some swag.

Psst… don’t let any of my colleagues know that I snagged four total items when they scrambled for singles: an Android shirt, an iOS shirt a Flutter shirt & a Flutter cup when I only had a third of the points they had.

To go even further beyond…

In reflection, I have no doubt that this internship is a defining moment in my career.

I learned the things I hoped to learn before entering the internship, such as learning the industry’s best practices, and getting familiar with a large codebase.

In addition, I got the chance to work on a real project and even publish some of my code into production.

I am genuinely thankful to all my mentors for sticking up with me and for me & for the opportunity to level up my knowledge.

I hope you enjoyed the story — you can find more of me here!
