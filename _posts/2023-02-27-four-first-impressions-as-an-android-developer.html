Four First Flutter Impressions as an Android Developer

Flutter. The word makes some feel euphoric. Others, tremble.

I got to develop with Flutter for a month. (Spoiler: I really enjoyed it!)

As a heads up, I will not weigh any upsides or downsides in comparison to native mobile application development.

Instead of that, I will share some personal impressions.

1. Are you kidding me? This formatting system is so good.

The feature of automatic formatting is such a game-changer!

It keeps the code beautifully flat and UI components can be separated so effortlessly with just a couple of key bindings.

Drop down some trailing commas, select a method with Shift+Up, extract it out with a Ctrl+Alt+M, and finish off with a Ctrl+Alt+L to reformat everything automagically.

I fell in love with the system, which is so elegant, and that’s exactly why I put it up in the first place.

2. Wow, I didn’t know I could build UI this fast.

I recall having to do a Collapsing Toolbar for the first time for a native Android app and it took me six hours to implement it properly.

I managed to perfect it in Flutter in 20 minutes because the component was built-in and easy to use.

In general, I’ve noticed myself building UI a lot faster in Flutter than in native.

Of course, I had already done it before, when I did it in Flutter, but this is just an example of how much faster things can really be!

I cannot skip mentioning the hot reload, the feature which allows the developer to see their code changes almost instantaneously.

3. Come on, Dart is simply less elegant compared to Kotlin.

This is not an attack towards Dart as a language! I repeat, this is not an attack towards Dart!

I think this one can really just be boiled down to Kotlin being so elegant and smooth.

The mandatory semicolons, constructor declarations, absence of data classes & other differences just make Flutter development a little bit less enjoyable for me.

But it’s not Dart’s fault, okay?

4. This dependency management with ‘dart pub’ is really simple to understand.

I’ve only tried this in a small app, but I thought it was much, much simpler than the Android dependency system for a beginner.

For instance, IDE support for .gradle files can be clunky sometimes, and for the .yaml ones, it all just works better out of the box.

Conclusion

I think native developers should definitely try Flutter, at least to see what it is about.

From what I’ve gathered, the technology has established itself as the go-to for small apps. (And some bigger ones).

The community is accepting, helping and it’s always beneficial to expand your knowledge as a developer, across all disciplines.

Personally, I have a positive view towards Flutter and while I enjoy native Android development more, Flutter has found some room in my heart too :)

I cannot end the story without thanking my TeleSoftas mentors who walked me through the onboarding.

They are absolute legends.
